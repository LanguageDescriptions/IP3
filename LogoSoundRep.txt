<h2>Representing two words in Logoori</h2>
<p>
On this page, we look at recordings of two words of Logoori (a Bantu language of Kenya), which translate to English ‘dog’ and ‘new’. The goal is to say exactly how these words are similar, and how they are different, which then tells us something about this language. We will work through a few ways of analyzing and representing the physical properties of these words, uncovering advantages and disadvantages of these methods.</p> 

<h3>Sound Recording</h3>
<br>
Any sound can be recorded on a computer and saved as a file of numbers, which can then be played back. Here we have an example of two words of Logoori. On all of these pages, you can press ‘play’ to hear the word. I recommend that you <b>do not</b> listen to these recordings right now, try valiantly to figure out the words based just on the numbers and pictures, then come back here to listen to see if you were right, after you try to interpret the spectrograms.

<h3>Reduction of sound to formant measurements</h3>

Next, we reduce the sound recordings to a mere 228 numbers each (listed below), so we can compare “F1 at 0.02 seconds in ‘dog’, versus F1 at 0.02 seconds in ‘new’.” The two words differ in F1 at that point in time by 32 Hz. An expert phonetician may eventually tell you something about the words from these numbers, but it requires a lot more specialized analysis of these 228 numbers: at this point, we just have a smaller wall of numbers. 

<p>

<h3 id="Wetware">Solution</h3> 
The problem was that we were trying to do completely different things with a single instrument. Many numeric tools can be used to discover the physical properties of sound (any sound at all), but we need a different set of tools to understand sounds linguistically: symbolic representations as they are used as discrete cognitive building blocks of language. We  reduce these two recordings to a tiny set of technically-defined symbols specifically developed for representing language sound – these words are [ɪ́mbwá] ‘dog’ and [ɪ́mbjá] ‘new’. How we do that is the topic of Chapter 2.
