A fieldworker may use various technological aids to emphasize a sound difference. One such technique is “slow listening”: mathematically stretch a sound file, making it easier to notice pitch changes that are obscured by the speed of normal speech

shortlong
lowerkosekato laughkoje:ɾato be allergic
higherkokeɾato milkkode:kato cook

Mathematical stretching may distort the underlying recording in undesirable ways (making it sound clearly mechanical). Sometimes speakers can do the same thing, so we can listen to EM deliberately stretching out the pronunciation of [kokeɾa, kode:ka, koseka, koje:ɾa].

shortlong
lowerkosekato laughkoje:ɾato be allergic
higherkokeɾato milkkode:kato cook

These consciously-stretched performances may allow the field worker to focus on the important locus of the pitch differences, because that is what the speaker is concentrating on (because we discussed what I was listening for). In listening to the slow versions, we see that in [koseka] the final syllable has less of a drop in pitch compared to [kokeɾa], and in [kode:ka] there is a more noticeable fall on the second half of the second syllable, whereas in [koje:ɾa], pitch falls a bit between the first and second, and between the second and third syllables. If you are having troubles reliably identifying these differences, remember that the purpose of this chapter is to explain the challenges that the field phonologist faces in creating the type of data that you will be analyzing in this course. A separate course of study and much practice may be necessary to be able to reliably transcribe tones.
